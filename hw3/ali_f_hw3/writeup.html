Part 1: Exploration with Tableau (20 points)

What is the relationship between operating system and numbers of years of programming experience?



The visualization uses small multiples with one multiple for each experience level (ordinal) encoded with y-position. For each experience level, the operating system (nominal) is encoded with y-position and color, and the number of students (quantitative) with a given amount experience using a given OS is encoded with line width. Windows XP is the most used operating system across all experience levels, with an increasing user base as the experience level rises. Mac OS and Windows Vista users are almost always equal across varying experience levels, and are most popular amongst students with 1 to 3 years of experience.

What concentrations do students who are more comfortable with programming come from?



The visualization above shows comfort level as different transformations. One is the original numeric comfort level (ordinal), while the other is a grouped view (ordinal), categorized as not comfortable (1 and 2), comfortable (3) and more comfortable (4 and 5). The raw comfort level is encoded with the height of line, the primary concentration is encoded with y-position and the number of students for each tuple is encoded with the length of the bar. This allows us to answer the question and dive into detail without any interaction. To answer the question, one need only compare the lengths of the dark blue lines. At the same time, the viewer can answer the question in more detail by drilling down into the ordinals within the "More comfortable" programmers. The visualization tells us that students who more comfortable with programming are studying are, in order: Computer Science, Information Management Systems, Economics, IT, Software Engineering, Applied Mathematics, Psychology, IT Management, Molecular and Cellular Biology, Digital Media and Electrical Engineering, to name most of them.

What programming languages do experienced programmers use?



To employ a different take on this question than the previous, I filtered out the less experienced programmers and only used those programmers with more than 3 years of programming experience. This allowed me to create a simple bar chart with minimal encoding (language with y-position, # of students using that language with length of the bar) and the ability to quickly answer the question. The languages experienced programmers use, in order, are: Java, C, PHP, Ruby, Python, C#, C++, HTML/CC, JavaScript, and VB/VBScript. The interesting thing to note is that there were some languages that were completely filtered out, meaning that there are no highly experienced programmers who consider the languages to be their primary development language.

What programming languages do experienced programmers who code the most often use?



This visualization adds an extra dimension to the last one and changes the encodings. The three data types are the experience level (ordinal), coding frequency (ordinal) and the programming language (nominal). Position is the best encoding for all data types, so I used x-position to encode the coding frequency, y-position to encode the experience,  color to encode the programming language, and size to encode the number of students who use a given language, code at a given frequency, and have a given amount of programming experience. As a result, the programmers who code most often and are most experienced bubble up to the top right of the chart, while the weakest and least-frequent programmers trickle down to the bottom left. Telling from the visualization, Java again takes the win, with C, C# and Python as runners up.

What is the relationship between age, programming language, and programming experience?



I used a small multiples visualization to answer this question, with one multiple per programming language (nominal). Age (ordinal) is encoded with y-position and color within a multiple and programming experience (ordinal) is encoded with x-position. x-position suits experience very well because it is also a temporal data type for which left to right flow of increasing time is intuitive. The number of students of a given age and experience level using a given programming language is encoded with the size. A connected line instead of data points is very effective because it exploits the connection Gestalt principles and allows us to better see increases and decreases (modern day humans are good at noticing small angles away from horizontal and vertical lines). The visualization tells us that most of the 18-24-year old students use mainstream languages like C, PHP, Java and Ruby, with most users within that group increasing with years of experience. We can also tell that Java's use is dominated by experienced 25-44-year old programmers, while C is dominated by 18-24-year old programmers with a variety of programming experience, most of whom have been programming for 1 to 3 years. PHP also has users with a good spread of programming experience. Python is dominated by 24-44-year old students with a fair amount of programming experience. It's also worth noting that dynamic languages like Ruby, Python and JavaScript are mostly used by experienced people. Some interesting outliers in the visualization are 1) the 65-year old C++ programmer, 2) the less experienced programmers who consider BASIC and SQL to be their primary programming language and the small clique of 18-24-year old VB/VBScript programmers with 1 to 3 years of experience.

 

Part 2: Designing an Interaction (40 points)

PDF slides | PowerPoint slides

The task I chose to address was identifying the relationship between the programming strengths of the students and their primary programming languages. I'm particularly interested in how programming strength plays a role in the choice between dynamically typed languages (e.g. Ruby and Python) and statically typed languages (e.g. C# and Java), but that is by no means the only sub-task that this visualization aims to address.


[Side note: Dynamically typed languages have recently started gaining popularity as expert programmers' weapon of choice. At the same time, traditional programmers maintain that compile-time type safety is better because it leaves less room for error. In my opinion, the situation is analogous to that of choosing between a sharp knife and a blunt one--stronger programmers feel confident to use sharper tools while weaker programmers and their technical managers feel the need for safer and more blunt tools at the cost of development speed. But of course there's a lot more to it than that.]

Based on the data, I narrowly define strength as a multidimensional data type because it encompasses a programmer's level of experience and how comfortable he/she is with programming. As such, the three dimensions of the visualization I designed are:


Programming language

Being a nominal data type, with instances such as Java, Perl, Python, C# and Ruby, it is spatially encoded with the x-position. Position is the best perceived encoding for all three data types and is used in the visualization to allow easy identification of the programming language when performing the task. Text labels are used to indicate the programming language at each x-position.

Comfort level with programming

Comfort level is an ordinal data type ranging from 1 to 5, with 5 being the most comfortable. For ordinal data types, the best encodings after position are density, saturation and hue. I chose to combine all three to encode it more generically as "color".  The lighter, yellower and lower saturation colors move up to darker, bluer and higher saturation colors as the comfort level increases. Comfort level is also encoded with the y-position (increasing with comfort level) when ordering two data points with the same experience. This allows the stronger of two equally experienced students to be shown as such.

Years of programming experience

This is also an ordinal data type with four instances: less than 6 months, between 6 months and 1 year, 1 to 3 years, and more than 3 years. The next best encoding available for nominal data types is textture, but given the requirement of encoding one of the data types with size (or more accurately, area), I encoded experience with circular area (or more effectively, diameter) as well as y-position. The more experienced the student, the greater the y value. The y-position encoding serves two purposes: 1) to be able to show all data points, and 2) to allow better perception of the ordinal.




Based on these encodings, the stronger programmers in class are the larger and darker blue circles at the top while the weaker ones are the smaller and yellower circles towards the bottom. In order to better explore the data, the visualization exploits the following interaction capabilities:


Additional detail on mouse over

When the user hovers their mouse over a circle representing a student, the following relevant details about the student are displayed in a tooltip:

    - How often the student writes code
    - What concentration they are studying at Harvard
    - What operating system they use

I chose to encode highlighted data with containment (3px border) and color (red). I chose red because studies indicate that red carries the strongest reaction of all the colors, with the level of reaction decreasing gradually with orange, yellow, and white, respectively. The change in brightness is also  easily detected by our peripheral vision, reducing the number of eye fixations required to absorb linked data points.

Based on my exploration of the visualization, I discovered several interesting themes:

1. The majority of the strongest programmers in our class use Java as their primary language. Runners up are C, PHP, Ruby, Python, C++ and C# users respectively. This could easily be attributed to the mainstream popularity of Java as a fundamental programming language.

2. Dynamic languages users primarily consist of experienced programmers.
3. The weakest programmers have mostly used BASIC, HTML/CSS, and SQL, which is not very surprising given that these languages are either archaic or peripheral languages (for example, most people wouldn't even call HTML/CSS a language.) In the case of SQL, this could also be due to the presence of database-administration type of students.
4. I was surprised to see C as being the most permeating language in the class. The spread of majors/concentrations in this group seems to be skewed towards Computer Science students but also has a small amount, yet wide variety, of nontraditional majors. Could this be because foundation-level programming classes at Harvard are taught in C?
